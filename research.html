Job market paper

M&As, Innovation and Superstar Firms · [Working Paper] · new draft coming soon

with M. Martinez-Cillero, L. Napolitano, F. Rentocchini, and E. Zaurino · JRC Working Papers on Corporate R&D and Innovation No. 3/2025, European Commission, Seville.

Abstract

Rising market concentration and the dominance of superstar firms have sparked concerns about declining competition and innovation. This paper investigates whether firms use technological M&As — acquisitions of innovative firms with patent portfolios — to enhance their market power. Using a global panel of 7,978 publicly listed firms across 80 countries from 2008 to 2020 and a staggered difference-in-differences design, we find that technological M&As raise acquirer markups by 1.45 percent on average — equivalent to approximately two years of the aggregate markup growth documented for the United States since 1980. Effects are stronger among top R&D investors, consistent with technological M&As amplifying the dominance of already-frontier firms, and are concentrated in high-tech manufacturing and knowledge-intensive services — sectors where proprietary technology constitutes a key strategic asset enabling differentiation and competitive advantage. Markup gains are substantially larger for deals involving digital and AI-related patents. Yet these gains are not accompanied by increased innovative activity or operational efficiency: post-deal patents filed by the merged entity do not increase, productivity declines in the short run before recovering, and fixed cost shares do not rise, ruling out R&D synergies, productive deployment of acquired intangibles, and economies of scale. Instead, markup gains are driven by the stock of patents acquired through the deal and are significantly amplified when technological and market proximity between the merging parties are high — consistent with firms strategically accumulating patents to fence off knowledge domains and shield themselves from rivals rather than to deploy acquired technologies productively. These findings highlight the need for antitrust policies that account for the innovation-shielding risks of technological M&As alongside standard efficiency considerations.

Skills and AI Innovative Ecosystems: Evidence from Brazilian Microregions · new draft coming soon

with R. Consentino de la Vega, F. Jaccoud, M. Menendez, and T. Ciarli

Abstract

This paper proposes a data-driven approach to identify the skills most strongly associated with the emergence of AI firms across Brazilian functional labour market regions (Regiões Geográficas Imediatas, RGIs). We combine worker-level employment records from RAIS with the ESCO skills taxonomy and firm-level data from FactSet/Crunchbase to construct a novel region–skill dataset covering nearly 13,500 skills across 392 RGIs. Using canonical correspondence analysis (CCA), we extract the latent skill dimensions that maximally correlate with AI firm presence, conditioning on regional income, the stock of digital firms, and broader digital innovation ecosystem factors. Across different model specifications, the skills most distinctively associated with AI firm presence remain stable, comprising foundational digital and organisational capabilities spanning software architecture, distributed computing, DevOps, cybersecurity, and advanced data platforms. These skills are geographically rare and concentrate in a small number of highly diversified regions, primarily major urban centres such as São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, Curitiba, and Brasília. These findings suggest that pre-existing digital human capital endowments shape the geography of AI entrepreneurship, and that the regional concentration of AI activity in Brazil reflects structural path dependencies rooted in accumulated skill bases.
